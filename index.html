Food Science

        

        
From the gut-brain axis to the Maillard reaction — the science behind what we eat and why it matters.

      


      

        
🧠 The Gut-Brain Axis

        

          
Modern nutrition research reveals a profound bidirectional communication network between the gut microbiome and the brain. The trillions of microorganisms in our digestive tract act as mediators between what we eat and how our brain functions.

          
Dietary patterns high in vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, and lean proteins are associated with reduced risk of cognitive decline, mild cognitive impairment, and Alzheimer's disease — driven by anti-inflammatory flavonoids and fiber fermentation metabolites.

          
Key insight: Bioavailability of these protective compounds is highly dependent on food combinations — the food matrix architecture determines how nutrients reach systemic circulation.

        

      


      

        
📊 The NOVA Framework

        
The most widely used food classification system in nutritional epidemiology, based on the degree of industrial processing.

        

          

            
Group 1 — Unprocessed

            Whole plants, fresh meats, eggs, milk
            
Minimal changes for preservation. Highest satiety. Reduced risk of chronic disease.

          

          

            
Group 2 — Culinary Ingredients

            Butter, olive oil, salt, sugar, flour
            
Extracted from Group 1. Used in home cooking. Calorie-dense but essential.

          

          

            
Group 3 — Processed Foods

            Canned beans, artisanal bread, simple cheeses
            
2-3 ingredients. Recognizable versions of original food. Generally healthy.

          

          

            
Group 4 — Ultra-Processed

            Industrial formulas with 5+ ingredients
            
Emulsifiers, HFCS, flavor enhancers. Linked to obesity, T2D, CVD. Disrupts food matrix.

          

        

      


      

        
🔥 Culinary Chemistry

        

          

            
The Maillard Reaction

            
A non-enzymatic browning reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars, beginning at ~140°C (284°F). Produces hundreds of flavor compounds and brown pigments (melanoidins). Responsible for the taste of roasted meats, baked bread, coffee, and seared steak.

            
Key: Needs both protein + sugar. Dry surface browns better (pat meat dry before searing).

          

          

            
Caramelization

            
Thermal decomposition of sugars in the absence of amino acids. Produces sweet, nutty, and bitter compounds with golden-brown color. Different starting sugars produce distinct flavors — sucrose vs. fructose vs. lactose each caramelize differently.

            
Key: Pure sugar transformation. Low moisture required. Temperature varies by sugar type.

          

          

            
Chocolate Tempering

            
Cocoa butter crystallizes in six polymorphic forms (I–VI). Only Form V (melting point 33.8°C) delivers the glossy finish, sharp snap, and mouth-melting properties of fine chocolate. Incorrect tempering leads to "bloom" — the gritty white Form VI surface.

            
Tempering: melt → cool to 27°C → rewarm to 31–32°C to seed only Form V crystals.

          

          

            
Fermentation Science

            
Soy sauce and miso rely on Aspergillus oryzae (koji) secreting proteases and amylases. Proteases hydrolyze proteins into glutamic acid → MSG in presence of salt. This creates umami. Lactic acid bacteria and yeasts develop complex secondary flavors over months.

            
Time = flavor depth. Longer fermentation = more complex umami compounds.

🏺 Traditional Food Science

        

          

            
Indigenous Australian Bush Tucker

            
Aboriginal Australians have practiced food science for 60,000+ years — the world's oldest continuous culinary tradition. Stone-ground Acacia seed flour dates to 65,000 years ago, making Indigenous Australians the world's first bakers.

            
Toxic seeds like Moreton Bay chestnut are detoxified via days of leaching in running water before baking — sophisticated safety science without modern labs.

          

          

            
Arctic Inuit Preservation

            
Igunaq: Walrus buried in permafrost to ferment over summer. Kiviaq: Hundreds of auk birds packed in seal skin and buried for months.

            
Vitamin C obtained from raw reindeer liver and seal brain — destroyed by heat. Natural refrigeration at -18°C halts glycogen depletion, providing more dietary glycogen than Western-slaughtered equivalents.

          

        

      

    


    
    

      

        
Food Origins

        

        
The world's food supply is built on ancient exchanges. 69% of the agricultural habits of 177 countries rely on crops that originated thousands of miles away.

      

      

        
Nikolai Vavilov's Centers of Origin (1920s) identified regions of greatest crop diversity as the birthplaces of domestication — now critical genetic reservoirs for climate resilience. Just 5.5% of the world's counties produce half the global food supply.
